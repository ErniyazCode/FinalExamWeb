Ancient and Medieval History

  • Stone Age Beginnings: Kazakhstan's habitation dates back to the Paleolithic era, evidenced by stone tools and cave paintings indicating early hunter-gatherer societies.
  • Nomadic Tribes Emergence Over time, the region became home to various nomadic tribes, primarily pastoralists herding sheep, goats, and cattle, moving seasonally for grazing.
  • Scythians and Huns Influence Notably, the Scythians (8th-3rd century BCE) and the Huns (4th-6th century CE) were known for their horseback riding and metalworking. Their kurgans (burial mounds) are rich in artifacts.
  • Cultural and Technological Evolution: These societies excelled in pastoralism and metalworking, shaping their lifestyle and social structure around nomadic practices.
  • Regional Impact: The movement of these tribes across Central Asia significantly influenced the region's cultural and historical development, setting the stage for future eras.

  • Cultural and Social Impact: The Mongol rule brought profound changes, integrating Kazakhstan into a vast empire that stretched across Eurasia. This period was marked by both turmoil and cultural exchanges.

Early Contacts and Incorporation into Russian Empire
  • 1731: The Little Zhuz, one of the three major Kazakh hordes (or "Zhuz"), begins the process of voluntary submission to the Russian Empire, seeking protection against the Dzungar Khanate.
  • Mid-18th Century: The Middle and Great Zhuzes gradually come under Russian influence, marking the beginning of Russian dominance in the region.
  • 1820s - 1840s: Russian policies intensify towards the Kazakh steppes, with the establishment of forts and relocation of Russian settlers, leading to tensions and uprisings.
Kazakh Resistance and Russian Consolidation
  • 1837-1847: The Kazakh leader Kenesary Kasymov leads a major uprising against Russian rule, symbolizing the resistance of the Kazakh people to foreign domination.
  • Mid-18th Century: The Middle and Great Zhuzes gradually come under Russian influence, marking the beginning of Russian dominance in the region.
  • Late 19th Century: Russian control is firmly established. The traditional nomadic lifestyle of the Kazakhs begins to change drastically due to Russian agricultural policies and the influx of settlers from Russia and Ukraine.
Impact on Kazakh Society and Culture
  • Early 20th Century: The region experiences significant social and economic changes. The Tsarist regime imposes Russification policies, affecting the cultural and linguistic landscape.
  • 1905 and 1917: Russian revolutions lead to political unrest and further transformations in Kazakhstan. The 1917 revolution especially triggers a period of turmoil and change, setting the stage for the Soviet era.

Soviet Era

The Soviet era in Kazakhstan was a period of profound transformation. It was marked by significant political, economic, and social changes that reshaped the country’s landscape, laying the foundation for its future independence and the challenges of post-Soviet nation-building.

Establishment of Soviet Power
  • 1917-1920: Following the Russian Revolution in 1917 and subsequent civil war, Soviet power is established in the region. Kazakhstan undergoes significant political and administrative restructuring.
  • 1920: Kazakhstan becomes an autonomous republic within the Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic.
Collectivization and Famine
  • Late 1920s - Early 1930s: The Soviet government under Joseph Stalin initiates aggressive collectivization policies, aiming to integrate individual landholdings and livestock into collective farms (kolkhozes) and state farms (sovkhozes).
  • 1931-1933: These policies lead to a devastating famine in Kazakhstan, causing the death of a significant portion of the Kazakh population and a mass exodus.
Industrialization and World War II
  • 1930s - 1940s: Kazakhstan experiences industrial growth, partly due to the Soviet industrialization policies. The discovery and development of mineral resources, including coal, oil, and metals, contribute to this growth.
  • World War II: During the war, Kazakhstan becomes a refuge for industries and people relocated from the European parts of the USSR. The region plays a crucial role in supplying resources for the Soviet war effort.
Cultural Changes and Russification
  • 1940s - 1980s: The Soviet era witnesses significant cultural and demographic shifts in Kazakhstan. The government promotes Russian language and culture, leading to a process of Russification. Despite this, Kazakh national consciousness continues to develop.
The Path to Sovereignty
  • 1986: The Jeltoqsan uprising in Almaty symbolizes growing nationalistic sentiments among the Kazakh population against the Soviet regime.
  • Late 1980s: Mikhail Gorbachev's policies of Perestroika and Glasnost create a political climate conducive to change, setting the stage for Kazakhstan's move towards independence.

Towards Independence

The period leading towards Kazakhstan's independence was characterized by a mix of political reforms, nationalism, and historic opportunities. It was a time of great change and hope, as Kazakhstan embarked on the path of self-determination and the building of a nation-state after centuries of foreign rule.
