Culture and Society in Pre-Independence Kazakhstan

Traditional Kazakh Lifestyle

Traditional Dress

Kazakhs wore clothing suited to their nomadic lifestyle. They donned thick robes, or "chapan," to protect against the harsh climate. Men typically wore high, pointed hats, while women adorned themselves with headscarves and elaborate jewelry. Clothing often indicated one's social status and was an important aspect of cultural identity.

Yurt - The Movable Home

The yurt is a symbol of the Kazakh nomadic heritage. These circular structures were made of a wooden frame covered in felt, making them both durable against the steppe climate and easily transportable. The interior of a yurt was practical yet ornate, decorated with handcrafted carpets and hangings known as "tuskiiz."

Diet and Cuisine

The nomadic diet was primarily meat and dairy-based. Traditional dishes included "beshbarmak" (boiled meat eaten with noodles and onion sauce), "kazy" (horse meat sausage), and "kumis" (fermented mare’s milk). These foods were well-suited to the nomadic lifestyle, providing high energy and ease of preparation.

Social Structure

Kazakh society was traditionally tribal, structured around extended family clans known as "ru." These clans were part of larger tribal unions that formed the three great hordes, or "Zhuz," which were the Senior, Middle, and Junior Zhuz. This social framework provided a support system and governed the rules of social conduct, marriage, and community responsibilities.

Cultural Significance of the Horse

The horse was central to Kazakh nomadic life, used for transportation, herding, and as a source of food and materials. The bond between Kazakhs and their horses was reflected in their folklore, songs, and the traditional sport of "kokpar," a type of polo played with a goat carcass.

Religious Practices

While Islam was the dominant religion, many Kazakhs retained elements of their pre-Islamic beliefs, such as Tengrism and shamanism. This syncretic approach to religion allowed for a variety of practices, such as ancestor worship and the use of talismans for protection.

Uly Zhuz

Geographical Location and Composition: The Senior Zhuz predominantly inhabited the southeastern regions of present-day Kazakhstan. This area is characterized by fertile lands and was historically significant for agriculture and trade.

Historical Role: Members of the Senior Zhuz were known for their involvement in trade, agriculture, and diplomacy. They often served as intermediaries between the Russian Empire and the Kazakh steppes, leveraging their strategic location and economic activities.

Cultural Contributions: The Uly Zhuz contributed significantly to the development of Kazakh culture, particularly in literature, music, and the arts. They maintained strong traditions in oral literature and were known for their skilled bards and storytellers.

Orta Zhuz

Geographical Location and Composition: The Middle Zhuz occupied the central steppe region of Kazakhstan. This area features vast grasslands, making it ideal for the nomadic pastoral lifestyle.

Historical Role: The Orta Zhuz were renowned for their skills in horse breeding and animal husbandry. They played a crucial role in maintaining the nomadic pastoral economy of the Kazakh people.

Cultural Contributions: The Middle Zhuz had a rich tradition of equestrian culture and were instrumental in preserving the traditional nomadic way of life, including yurt living, horsemanship, and oral traditions.

Kishi Zhuz

The Junior Zhuz resided primarily in the western and northern parts of Kazakhstan, areas characterized by harsher climates and less fertile lands.

Historical Role: The Kishi Zhuz were known for their resilience and adaptability to challenging environments. They were skilled in fishing and hunting, supplementing their pastoral lifestyle.

Cultural Contributions: The Junior Zhuz contributed to the diversity of Kazakh culture through their unique customs and practices. They have a distinct set of folk songs, dances, and oral traditions that reflect their adaptation to their environment.
